## 1. The NOSE Structure

The single most important structural framework for persuasive proposals. Write all proposals in this order:

| Element | What It Does | Common Error |
|---|---|---|
| **N** — Need | State the client's business problem clearly and specifically — this is the opening | Opening with company history or the agency's capabilities |
| **O** — Outcome | Describe the results the client seeks — the payoff from solving the problem | Describing your process instead of the client's benefit |
| **S** — Solution | Your specific recommendation — what you will do, how, and why it fits this client | Presenting the solution before establishing the need |
| **E** — Evidence | Proof: case studies, data, testimonials — positioned to support the solution | Leading with credentials before the need is established |

**The most common structural error:** Starting with the agency's history, capabilities, or credentials before establishing the client's need. The client's name should appear two to three times more often than the agency's name in the executive summary.

**The primacy test:** Read only your executive summary. Does it open with the client's problem? Does the client's name appear 2–3× more than yours? If not, rewrite.

## 2. The Seven Magic Questions

Answer these before writing a single word of the proposal:
1. What is the client's specific problem or need?
2. Why is this problem worth solving? (the business driver — why now?)
3. What results does the client seek?
4. Which specific result is the most important to them?
5. What services can I offer to solve the problem and deliver results?
6. Of those services, which is the best fit for this specific client?
7. Why are we the right choice — and not a competitor?

**Chain of pain diagnostic:** Keep asking "why?" until you reach the root business problem, not the surface request. The stated need is rarely the actual problem.

## 3. The Seven Deadly Sins of Proposal Writing

Self-assess before sending any proposal — fail on any of these and the proposal loses:
1. No focus on the client's business problems and payoffs — the proposal is about the agency, not the client
2. No persuasive structure — the proposal is an information dump without an argument
3. No clear differentiation from competitors — why us specifically?
4. No compelling value proposition — what outcome justifies the investment?
5. No impact or highlighting — key points are buried in paragraphs
6. No orientation to the audience — too long, too technical, wrong vocabulary
7. No credibility — spelling errors, wrong client name, inconsistent formatting

## 4. The Fluff/Guff/Geek/Weasel Test

Eliminate these four categories from every proposal before sending:

| Category | Definition | Examples to remove |
|---|---|---|
| **Fluff** | Empty marketing superlatives with no evidential basis | "world-class", "innovative solutions", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class" |
| **Guff** | Pompous, bureaucratic language that obscures meaning | "pursuant to", "as per our discussion", "in accordance with", "herewith" |
| **Geek** | Technical jargon the client does not use | Platform acronyms, metric names without plain-English explanation |
| **Weasel** | Hedging language that signals low confidence | "we believe", "we hope to", "we would endeavour to", "subject to" |

Replace with: active voice, specific numbers, the client's own language, concrete commitments.

## 5. The Primacy Principle

What appears first in a document creates the frame through which everything else is evaluated. If the executive summary opens with the agency's history, the reader concludes you are self-centred — and that impression persists even if subsequent content is fully client-focused.

**Rule:** Open every document with the client's problem. Every time. Without exception.

## 6. The Four Maxims of Persuasive Communication (Grice)

Apply these to every sentence:
1. **Quality** — tell the truth; only assert what you can evidence
2. **Quantity** — provide only as much detail as the audience needs to decide; less is almost always better
3. **Relation** — every statement must be relevant to the audience's decision, not to the writer's agenda
4. **Manner** — be clear; avoid jargon, ambiguity, and unnecessary complexity

**Trust equation:** Trust = (Your Expertise × Knowledge of Client's Business) ÷ Perceived Risk

## 7. The Persuasion Sandwich (Hatton)

Structure every written proposal as:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Cover / title | First impression — physical evidence of service quality and professionalism |
| Executive summary | Client's problem stated first; solution headline; key benefit — the proposal in miniature |
| Understanding of the requirement | Demonstrate you listened; restate their need in their language |
| Proposed solution | Benefits-led; structured around their desired outcomes, not your methodology |
| Credentials / evidence | Case studies positioned to prove the solution — placed here, not first |
| Commercials | Pricing in context of value, not as a bare number |
| Next steps / close | Clear, specific, and easy to say yes to |

## 8. The Competitive Advantage Equation (Hatton)

Competitive advantage = delivering more relevant benefits than alternatives, as perceived by the buyer.

Value for money = (Product + Place + Promotion + People + Processes + Physical Evidence) ÷ Price

**Implication:** The proposal document itself is Physical Evidence. Its quality, clarity, and precision signal the quality of your service delivery. A poorly formatted proposal communicates: "Our work will look like this."

---

## 9. Go/No-Go Qualifying Criteria (Hatton)

Before investing time in a full proposal, answer yes to all three:
1. Do we want this business? (Is it profitable, strategic, and repeatable?)
2. Can we win it? (Do we have a genuine competitive advantage for this client?)
3. Can we deliver it? (Resources, capability, and current capacity?)

A strong "no" decision is as strategically important as a winning proposal — declining a wrong-fit client frees capacity for the right one.
