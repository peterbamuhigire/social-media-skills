*The definitive tone and identity reference for anyone writing, designing, or briefing content for Maduuka. Read this before writing a single word or briefing a single graphic.*

---

## Section 1: Brand Voice Guide

### Tone Attributes

The three confirmed tone adjectives are: **Sharp. Grounded. Empowering.**

---

**Attribute 1: Sharp — Precise without being cold**

*Definition:* Maduuka's content says exactly what it means using the fewest words necessary. It does not pad, hedge, or use five words where two will do. Sharpness is not harshness — it is the quality of language that respects a business owner's time and intelligence.

*Right:*
- "Stock leaving with no record? EFRIS penalty coming with no warning. Maduuka fixes both."
- "Your cashier rang up 47 sales today. Your register says 44. Maduuka shows you which three."

*Wrong:*
- "In today's fast-paced and increasingly competitive business environment, it has never been more important for SMEs to leverage cutting-edge technology solutions to streamline their operational efficiency."
- "We are pleased to introduce our innovative and comprehensive business management platform designed to help your business thrive."

---

**Attribute 2: Sharp — One idea per post**

*Definition:* Every piece of content has a single point. Not two points. Not "and also." If a post is trying to say everything, it communicates nothing. This applies equally to captions, ad copy, WhatsApp messages, and LinkedIn posts.

*Right:*
- A post about EFRIS. Full stop. No mention of stock management.
- A post about drug expiry losses at a pharmacy. Not drug expiry AND stock management AND EFRIS.

*Wrong:*
- "Maduuka handles your stock, your sales, your invoicing, your EFRIS compliance, your staff management, and your financial reports — all in one place."
- "Are you struggling with stock management, compliance requirements, staff oversight, and financial tracking? We have the solution."

---

**Attribute 3: Grounded — Built for here, not adapted for here**

*Definition:* Maduuka's voice comes from the Uganda and East Africa business context, not from a Western software playbook. It names real Kampala problems, references the real regulatory environment (EFRIS, NDA, URA), and uses language that a Ugandan business owner uses. It never sounds like a foreign product that has added "Africa" to its tagline.

*Right:*
- "Most Kampala shops lose 15–20% of their stock to theft and poor record-keeping every month. Not because of bad staff. Because of no system."
- "EFRIS is not optional. URA inspectors are coming. Here is what you need to have ready."

*Wrong:*
- "Global best-in-class inventory management for the modern African enterprise."
- "Empowering African businesses to achieve operational excellence and digital transformation."

---

**Attribute 4: Grounded — Trusts the audience**

*Definition:* Maduuka talks to business owners who are running real operations, managing real money, and making real decisions. They do not need to be told that "business is important." They need to be told something they do not already know, or reminded of something they are avoiding. Treat the audience as competent adults with limited time.

*Right:*
- "You already know you need a system. Here is the specific thing that makes Maduuka worth trying first."
- "We will not pretend this is simple to set up. It takes about two hours the first time. What it saves you after that is a Sunday afternoon every month."

*Wrong:*
- "Running a business is hard! That is why Maduuka is here to help you every step of the way."
- "Do you know how much your business could grow with the right tools? Find out today!"

---

**Attribute 5: Empowering — The business owner is the hero**

*Definition:* Maduuka is the tool. The business owner is the protagonist. Every piece of content should make the reader feel capable, not dependent. Empowering content gives the audience something they can use — knowledge, a framework, a decision — and positions Maduuka as the thing that makes their capability possible.

*Right:*
- "With Maduuka, you are the one who knows. Not your cashier. Not your manager. You."
- "Here is the report your accountant has been asking for. You can generate it yourself in 3 minutes."

*Wrong:*
- "Let Maduuka take care of everything so you can focus on what matters."
- "Leave the hard work to us — we handle your business so you do not have to."

---

**Attribute 6: Empowering — Celebrates the business, not the software**

*Definition:* Case studies, testimonials, and success posts celebrate what the business owner achieved. Maduuka is mentioned as part of the story, not the headline. This mirrors how Shopify communicates — the merchant is the star; the tool is the enabler.

*Right:*
- "Amina opened her second restaurant this month. She started tracking everything properly 90 days ago. This is her story."
- "Patrick's stock count used to take his whole Sunday. Now it takes 20 minutes. Here is what changed."

*Wrong:*
- "Maduuka helped Patrick reduce stock count time by 90%. Our software delivers real results."
- "Another success story — Maduuka transforms another Kampala business."

---

### Brand Voice Summary Paragraph

Maduuka sounds like the most useful person in the room — the one who has actually run a shop in Kampala, dealt with a URA inspector, and come out the other side with a system that works. The brand speaks plainly, names problems by their real names, and never dresses up a sales message as advice. It is warm in the way that East African professional culture is warm — respectful, direct, and interested in the person it is talking to — but it wastes no words. When Maduuka explains something, the explanation is the right length and no longer. When it celebrates a client, it names the client's achievement, not its own. It never says "game-changing," "revolutionary," or "world-class." It says what happened, what it cost, and what was gained. If this brand were a person walking into a meeting with a Kampala business owner, it would be the kind of person who has already read the business owner's accounts before walking in, knows exactly what question to ask first, and leaves the business owner feeling capable rather than sold to.

## Section 2: Platform Tone Adjustments

*Source announcement for worked examples:* "Maduuka is now available on Android and web. Kampala business owners can sign up for a free trial at maduuka.com."

---

**LinkedIn**

Tone: Professional, insight-led, peer-to-peer. Maduuka on LinkedIn speaks as a company that understands the professional side of running a business — compliance, financial management, systems. The audience includes other professionals, potential B2B partners, and business owners who self-identify as professionals.

Rules:
- Lead with a relevant observation or problem before the announcement. Never open with the product name.
- Full sentences. No bullet point lists masquerading as insight.

Example:
> "Most small business owners in Uganda are running EFRIS compliance on a manual system that will not hold up to an audit. URA enforcement is increasing. We built Maduuka specifically to handle EFRIS for retail, hospitality, and pharmacy — built in, not bolted on. Free trial now live at maduuka.com. (Link in first comment.)"

---

**Facebook**

Tone: Warm, community-first, conversational. Maduuka on Facebook talks to the same business owner who is in the market, on the street, running a real operation. The register is friendly and practical. Facebook is where Maduuka reaches Patrick the retailer and Amina the restaurant owner — not through corporate polish but through content that speaks their language.

Rules:
- Open with the problem or the observation, not the product name.
- Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Mobile-optimised.

Example:
> "Every week, we talk to Kampala business owners who are losing stock they cannot account for. Not because they are careless. Because they have no system that catches it.
>
> Maduuka is now live on Android and web. Sign up for a free trial and see exactly what is in your shop — and what is leaving without a record.
>
> Link in the first comment. 👇"

---

**Instagram**

Tone: Visual-first, direct, grounded. The caption supports a strong visual — a real Kampala shop, a real dashboard screenshot, a real person. The first line must work before the "see more" button.

Rules:
- First line is the hook. It must earn the tap.
- Keep it short. The image does the heavy lifting.
- Hashtags at the end or in the first comment.

Example:
> "Know what's in your shop. Know what sold. Know what's missing.
>
> Maduuka is live on Android and web. Free trial at maduuka.com — link in bio.
>
> #MaduukaApp #KampalaBusinessOwner #EFRISCompliance"

---

**WhatsApp (Business Broadcast)**

Tone: Personal, direct, one-to-one. WhatsApp is a private channel. It should feel like a message from someone who knows the recipient's business situation — not a broadcast from a marketing department.

Rules:
- Never open with the product name or a promotional hook.
- Lead with something useful or personally relevant.
- One call to action maximum.

Example:
> "Hi — we wanted to let you know that Maduuka is now available for Android and web. If you are managing stock or EFRIS compliance manually, the free trial is worth 20 minutes of your time. Here is the link: [link]"

---

**X / Twitter**

Tone: Sharp, opinionated, one idea. X is where Maduuka engages in business and tech discourse. The register is the most direct of any platform — one strong point, no softening.

Rules:
- Cut every word that is not working.
- Take a clear position. "It depends" posts go nowhere.

Example:
> "Most Kampala business owners are using WhatsApp as their stock management system.
>
> It is not a stock management system.
>
> Maduuka is live. Free trial: maduuka.com"

## Section 3: Vocabulary List

### Words and Phrases to Use

| Word / Phrase | Why it fits |
|---|---|
| Know what you have | Grounds the value proposition in the business owner's lived anxiety — uncertainty about stock |
| EFRIS-ready | Immediately signals compliance without jargon; answers the question SMEs are actually asking |
| Built for African business | Distinguishes Maduuka from adapted Western tools without being performative |
| Track it | Plain, active, direct — reflects the Sharp voice |
| Real-time | Clear and immediately understood; no synonym is needed |
| Your shop / your restaurant / your pharmacy | Persona-specific language that speaks to the actual business context |
| Closing time report | Specific to the business owner's experience — the moment they review what happened |
| Before you lock up | Grounded in the physical reality of running a shop or restaurant |
| Stock count | The specific term Ugandan retailers use; prefer over "inventory management" |
| Demo | Direct; no reason to say "complimentary discovery session" |
| Trial | Honest and low-commitment framing; prefer over "free plan" or "onboarding" |
| Built here | Short, powerful, distinguishes from "launched in Africa by a foreign company" |
| See exactly | Precision language; pairs well with the Sharp attribute |
| Week-ending report | Practical and time-anchored; speaks to how business owners actually review their numbers |
| Penalty | Honest; conveys EFRIS risk without softening |

---

### Words and Phrases to Avoid

| Word / Phrase | Why to avoid |
|---|---|
| Game-changing | Listed explicitly in the EA avoid list; overused to the point of meaninglessness |
| Revolutionary | Same as above — hollow claim that triggers scepticism |
| World-class | Foreign-sounding; implies the standard is elsewhere, not here |
| Enterprise-grade | Sounds expensive and inaccessible; not relevant to SME positioning |
| Cutting-edge | Corporate filler; says nothing specific |
| Leverage | Ugly business jargon; no Ugandan SME owner uses this word about their own business |
| Seamless | The default word of every software company; signals lazy copy |
| Streamline | Same problem as seamless — everyone says it; nobody believes it anymore |
| Empower (as a standalone claim) | Works as a tone attribute but becomes hollow as a content word: "We empower businesses" says nothing |
| Solutions | Generic business-speak; always replace with what the solution actually does |
| Digital transformation | Aspirational corporate language; alienates the target SME audience |
| Innovative | Claims innovation without evidence; the product should demonstrate it, not announce it |
| Amazing / awesome | Informal American exclamation; not in the EA professional register |
| Unlock your potential | Aspirational phrasing that asks nothing of the audience; too soft for the Sharp voice |
| One-stop-shop | Cliché; signals that the copy is not thinking about the audience specifically |

## Section 4: Emoji Policy

Maduuka's tone is Sharp, Grounded, and Empowering. Emoji use is minimal and functional — they direct or punctuate, they do not decorate. A business owner reading Maduuka content should feel the brand is composed and credible. Excessive emoji use undermines that.

**Platform-by-platform emoji usage:**

| Platform | Emojis used? | Frequency | Types appropriate | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn | Sparingly | Maximum 1 per post | Functional only (➡️ ✅ 📌) | Only where it replaces a bullet point or directs attention; never decorative |
| Facebook | Yes | 1–2 per post | Functional + one expressive | A 👇 to direct to a link is appropriate; avoid celebratory decorative use |
| Instagram | Yes | 2–3 per caption | Functional + expressive | Hashtag section can include one relevant emoji; keep captions clean |
| WhatsApp | Yes | 1 per message maximum | Functional | A 👇 at the end of a message pointing to a link is appropriate; nothing else |
| X / Twitter | No / rarely | 0–1 per post | Functional only if used | The Sharp voice works best without emoji on X; only use if it adds meaning |

**Emoji types:**
- Functional emojis (✅ ➡️ 📌 👇 🔗): **Permitted** — use where they direct attention or replace a word efficiently
- Expressive emojis (😊 🙌 👏): **Limited** — maximum one per post; only where warm acknowledgement is the specific intent
- Decorative emojis (🌟 ✨ 💫 🎉): **Not permitted** — these read as filler and undermine the Sharp voice

**Emojis to avoid:**
- 🔥 — overused; feels like every Ugandan telecom ad
- 💯 — informal and aggressive; not in Maduuka's register
- 🚀 — startup cliché; this is precisely the Western SaaS hype style we are avoiding
- 😂 — too casual for a business credibility brand
- 🌍 — "African" trope; performative rather than grounded
- ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ — review-baiting aesthetics; undermines the tone
- 💪 — motivational poster aesthetic; not Maduuka's register

## Section 5: Hashtag Brand Identity

### Branded Hashtags

**#Maduuka**
- *Purpose:* The primary brand hashtag — used on all organic content across all platforms
- *Platforms:* LinkedIn, Instagram, X, Facebook (where hashtags are relevant)
- *Usage rule:* Every post
- *How to encourage audience use:* Invite clients to post about their experience with #Maduuka. Feature any customer who uses it in a Story or repost.

**#MaduukaApp**
- *Purpose:* Secondary brand hashtag for product-specific posts — demos, tutorials, feature announcements
- *Platforms:* Instagram and X primarily
- *Usage rule:* Product-specific posts only; not every post
- *How to encourage audience use:* Use it consistently in product content; it will build association over time

**#BuiltForAfrica** *(candidate — check availability)*
- *Purpose:* Positioning hashtag for Big Picture content about building software for the African context
- *Platforms:* LinkedIn and X primarily
- *Usage rule:* Big Picture and thought leadership posts; not promotional content
- *Note:* Search this on Instagram and LinkedIn before publishing — if it is already in wide use, replace with #BuiltInUganda or a variant

---

### Community and Discovery Hashtags

**Industry hashtags (rotate based on content topic):**
- #EFRISCompliance — high-relevance, specific to Uganda regulatory content
- #SMEUganda — broad reach across Ugandan small business community
- #BusinessManagement — wider discovery; use sparingly alongside more specific tags

**Location hashtags:**
- #KampalaBusiness — primary location tag; used on posts targeting Kampala SME owners
- #UgandaBusiness — broader geographic reach when content applies nationally
- #EastAfricaBusiness — use when content is relevant to the broader EA market

**Platform guidance:**
- Instagram: 3–5 hashtags per post; place at end of caption or in first comment
- LinkedIn: 3 hashtags maximum; embedded in post (not appended as a list)
- X: 1–2 hashtags maximum; integrated naturally into the post text
- Facebook: Hashtags have minimal effect; use branded hashtag only if used at all

Branded hashtags are permanent. Community hashtags rotate based on content. Trending hashtags are time-limited — review weekly and only use when genuinely relevant.

## Section 6: Visual Identity Brief

*For briefing the designer or content creator. This document does not produce design files. Hand this section directly to the person creating Maduuka's visual assets.*

---

### Photography Style Direction

**Subject matter:** Real Ugandan business owners in their actual business environments — shops, restaurants, pharmacy counters. The product on a real Android device held by real hands in a real business context. Dashboard screenshots on mobile screens.

**People in images:** Yes — essential. Use real clients wherever possible (with permission). Staff can be used for behind-the-scenes content. Models should be selected to reflect the actual Maduuka customer demographic: Ugandan, 28–50, business-owner context. Always candid or lightly directed — never stiff posed corporate photography.

**Setting:** Real Kampala business environments: a retail shop shelf, a restaurant kitchen pass, a pharmacy dispensing counter, a market stall owner looking at a phone. Never a generic office, boardroom, or co-working space. The product must appear in context.

**Mood:** Confident and grounded. Not aspirational (that is Flutterwave territory). Not clinical (that is Sage territory). The mood is: this is a real business owner in a real situation with a real solution. Warm lighting, natural settings, authentic expressions.

**Professional vs. authentic:** Balanced — 60% authentic/real, 40% polished. The goal is credibility, not perfection. An image that looks slightly real is always more trustworthy than one that looks fully produced.

**Stock photography policy:** Avoid stock photography for people and business contexts. The EA demographic is poorly served by stock libraries — the people do not look like Maduuka's customers, and the business settings do not look like Kampala. Stock is acceptable for abstract UI illustration or technology metaphors only; never for human or business-context images.

---

### Colour Palette Usage

| Colour role | Colour (hex or description) | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Primary | #0054a6 (deep professional blue) | Main brand colour — dominant in all content; backgrounds, headers, CTA buttons |
| Secondary | White (#FFFFFF) | Supporting — text on blue, clean backgrounds, space |
| Accent | To be confirmed — suggest a warm amber or green as a high-contrast CTA colour | CTA buttons, highlight callouts, notification elements |
| Dark background | Deep navy or charcoal (#0a1628 suggested) | Dark-mode graphics, night-mode visuals, premium feel |
| Text on dark | White (#FFFFFF) | All text overlaid on dark or blue backgrounds |
| Text on light | Dark grey (#1a1a2e or equivalent) | Body text on light backgrounds — softer than pure black |

**Colours to avoid:**
- Red — associated with errors and warnings in UI; avoid in brand identity contexts
- Generic green — overused in African tech and finance brands (M-Pesa, Equity); differentiation risk
- MTN yellow or Airtel red — immediate association with telecom brand style we are explicitly avoiding

---

### Typography Guidance

**Heading font:** Poppins Bold / SemiBold — personality: modern, warm, approachable but structured. Works well for post headlines and graphic headers.

**Body font:** Inter Regular / Medium — personality: clean, highly legible at small sizes, technically precise. Ideal for caption text, dashboard UI references, and any text in graphics.

**On social media graphics:**
- Headlines: Poppins SemiBold or Bold at 28pt minimum
- Subtext or supporting copy: Inter Regular at 18pt minimum
- For Instagram captions and LinkedIn post text: Inter or system-default; no custom font rendering needed (these are text posts)

**Font size guidance:** Minimum 24px for any text on social graphics. Text smaller than this is unreadable on mobile screens. All social graphics are consumed on mobile first.

**Fonts to avoid:**
- Comic Sans — obvious
- Brush Script or any decorative handwriting fonts — not in keeping with Sharp, Grounded voice
- Condensed or ultra-thin fonts — illegible on mobile
- Default PowerPoint or Canva fonts (Calibri, Arial, Montserrat as default) — these read as uncrafted; Poppins/Inter combination is distinctive without being unusual

---

### Image Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Use real Ugandan business owners in real Kampala settings wherever possible
- Show the product on an actual Android phone in context — a hand holding a phone with the Maduuka interface visible is better than a floating app screenshot
- Use #0054a6 blue as the dominant brand signal in every graphic — colour consistency builds recognition fast
- Show numbers and data in graphics — a specific stat or dashboard reading makes the value proposition concrete
- Use before/after image pairs — handwritten stock book versus a clean Maduuka dashboard; this contrast communicates the transformation immediately

**Do not:**
- Use stock photography of people who do not reflect the Ugandan and East African demographic of Maduuka's customers
- Use images that look like they belong in a corporate annual report — boardrooms, handshakes, glass office buildings
- Place small text on busy photographic backgrounds — unreadable on mobile
- Use more than two font weights in a single graphic — visual noise undermines the Sharp identity
- Add the Maduuka logo as a watermark over people's faces or in awkward positions — brand placement should be clean and intentional, not stamped on content
