## Persona 1: Patrick Kiggundu — "The Stretched Retailer"

**Archetype:** The business owner who outgrew his system without realising it

---

**Demographics**

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Name | Patrick Kiggundu |
| Age | 34–44 |
| Gender | Male (though female retail owners are common; gender is not a differentiating factor for content strategy — content should not be gender-coded) |
| Location | Kampala — Ntinda, Kireka, Nateete, or Kikuubo area; runs a general merchandise, electronics, or phones and accessories shop |
| Income level | UGX 2.5M–6M per month (revenue); take-home after expenses estimated UGX 800K–2M — *Assumption: based on typical Kampala mid-level retail operation; validate with real customer data* |
| Education | Certificate or diploma; possibly a business degree from a private university |
| Occupation | Owner-operator of a single retail shop; may employ 1–3 staff |
| Household | Married or in a relationship; supporting extended family; owns or renting in a middle-income area |

---

**Platform Usage**

| Platform | Uses it? | Frequency | Peak time | How they use it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp | Yes — daily | Multiple times per day | 6am–9pm | Primary business communication: supplier orders, customer enquiries, staff coordination, family. Business and personal mixed on one number. |
| Facebook | Yes | Daily scrolling, occasional posting | Evenings 7–9pm, lunch 12–1pm | Business pages for product photos; personal for entertainment, sports, news, church/community groups |
| Instagram | Occasionally | 2–3 times per week | Evenings | Browsing products from suppliers; not actively posting for business |
| TikTok | Sometimes | Casual consumption | Evenings | Entertainment only — not business-relevant |
| LinkedIn | No / rarely | — | — | Not part of his current digital behaviour |
| YouTube | Yes | Several times per week | Evenings and weekends | How-to videos, music, news highlights. Searches "how to do EFRIS" type content |
| X / Twitter | No | — | — | Not active |

**Most reachable on:** Facebook (broad SME owner content) and YouTube (how-to and tutorial search). WhatsApp for direct business communication once he is in the funnel.

---

**Content He Engages With**

1. **Problem-specific how-to content** — "How to reduce stock theft in your shop without cameras" — practical and immediately applicable to his daily stress
2. **EFRIS explainers** — Short video or carousel explaining what EFRIS requires and what happens if a business does not comply; he is anxious about this and searches for it
3. **Business owner stories from Kampala** — Content featuring real Ugandan retailers talking about their experience. Social proof from someone who looks and sounds like him is worth more than any testimonial from a corporate client
4. **Before/after comparisons** — Posts showing what managing stock used to look like (WhatsApp messages, handwritten books) versus what it looks like now (clean dashboard, reports on a phone). The contrast is visceral and recognisable.
5. **Cost and risk content** — Posts that quantify the cost of not having a proper system: "The average Kampala shop loses UGX X per month to untracked stock." Numbers that map to his situation get his attention.

---

**Pain Points**

1. **Stock disappears and he cannot prove how.** He suspects staff but has no clear audit trail. Manual stock counts take time and are never fully accurate. He cannot confidently know whether losses are from theft, poor record-keeping, or genuine waste.

2. **EFRIS compliance is a constant low-grade anxiety.** He has heard it is required. He has heard penalties are coming. He does not fully understand what he needs to do or whether his current setup (if any) is compliant. He would rather pay someone to handle it than try to understand it himself — but he cannot afford to be blindly dependent.

3. **He cannot manage the shop when he is away from it.** When he leaves to go to the bank, see a supplier, or attend to something else, he is flying blind. He has no real-time view of what is happening, what has been sold, or what staff are doing.

---

**Goals**

1. Stop losing stock to theft and poor record-keeping — he wants to run a business where the numbers actually add up at the end of the month.
2. Become and stay EFRIS-compliant without it consuming his attention — he wants this handled, not explained.
3. Open a second branch or at least have the systems that would make a second branch possible within the next two years.

---

**Triggers to Follow a Brand**

- A Facebook post appears in his feed that names a problem he recognises exactly — "If your stock count is always wrong..." He clicks because it describes his reality.
- A business owner he respects in his network has liked or shared the page. Social proof from his peer group matters more than advertising.
- The content is practical and free — it gives him something useful without asking him to buy first. He follows brands that help him first.
- The brand posts in a register that matches how he talks — not corporate English, not condescending, but peer-to-peer and grounded.

---

**Triggers to Unfollow a Brand**

- Three promotional posts in a row with no useful content. His feed is already full of people trying to sell him things.
- Content that talks down to him or assumes he does not know basic business concepts.
- Inconsistent posting — he follows a brand expecting regular content, checks back two weeks later and finds nothing new.
- Generic international stock photography — feels foreign and unrelatable.

---

**How Maduuka Fits His Life**

Patrick runs his shop six days a week. By Wednesday, the energy of a fresh Monday morning has faded into the grind of managing stock disputes with his assistant, chasing a supplier who delivered the wrong quantity, and trying to remember whether he reconciled last Friday's sales. He does not have time to implement a new system — every day is full. But he also knows, quietly, that the system he currently has is not a system: it is hope. When Maduuka works for Patrick, it does not feel like software. It feels like finally knowing. Knowing what he has. Knowing what he sold. Knowing that the number on his phone matches what should be on the shelf. When a URA inspector comes in, he does not panic. When a staff member claims he did not receive a delivery, Patrick can check. That peace — the knowledge that the numbers exist and are correct — is what Maduuka is selling. Not software. Not a subscription. Peace of mind and control.

---

**Messaging That Resonates**

- **Tone:** Direct, peer-to-peer, occasionally frank — as if advice from a fellow business owner who figured something out
- **Vocabulary:** Plain Ugandan business language; occasional Luganda word or phrase lands well; avoids British corporate vocabulary ("operational efficiency," "streamline workflows"); prefers "know what you have" over "real-time inventory visibility"
- **Platforms:** Facebook for reach; WhatsApp for conversion; YouTube for educational depth
- **Content style:** Short punchy captions, strong real-world visual hook (an actual Kampala shop interior, not a stock photo), one clear point per post
- **Proof type:** Real Ugandan retailer testimonials with specific numbers; before/after dashboards from actual use; endorsements from recognisable Kampala business figures

---

**Messaging to Avoid**

- Generic stock photography of a smiling stock boy in a foreign supermarket — looks nothing like his reality
- "World-class" or "enterprise-grade" — sounds foreign and expensive, not relevant
- Comparisons to Sage, QuickBooks, or SAP — he has not heard of most of them and the comparison means nothing
- Overly complex product feature lists — he does not buy features, he buys outcomes

---

**Assumptions Flag**

> *Assumption: Income level estimated from typical Kampala mid-level retail operation norms. Validate against actual first paying clients.*
> *Assumption: EFRIS anxiety assumed from publicly available URA enforcement news and common SME conversations; validate with real client interviews.*
> *Assumption: Platform usage pattern based on Uganda digital behaviour research and EA platform defaults; first client WhatsApp conversations will surface actual patterns quickly.*

## Persona 2: Amina Nakato — "The Restaurant Owner Under Pressure"

**Archetype:** The hospitality entrepreneur whose business has outpaced her tools

*Note: A hospitality business owner is already on the Maduuka waitlist. This persona is based on that signal, enriched with market knowledge.*

---

**Demographics**

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Name | Amina Nakato |
| Age | 28–40 |
| Gender | Female (women-owned restaurants and bars are prominent in Kampala's hospitality sector; gender is relevant here because the content and tone should reflect a female business owner's experience and peer network) |
| Location | Kampala — Kisementi, Kololo, Bugolobi, Ntinda, or Mengo area; runs a mid-range restaurant, bar, or restaurant-bar hybrid |
| Income level | UGX 3M–8M per month (business revenue); personal take-home after expenses and staff wages: UGX 1M–3M — *Assumption: based on typical Kampala mid-range restaurant turnover; validate with real data* |
| Education | Diploma in hospitality or business management; or self-taught through running the business |
| Occupation | Owner-manager of a restaurant or bar; employs 3–8 staff (waiters, cooks, cashier) |
| Household | May be supporting family; renting in a middle-income area; some ambition to expand or franchise |

---

**Platform Usage**

| Platform | Uses it? | Frequency | Peak time | How they use it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp | Yes — constant | All day | Operating hours 8am–11pm | Takes orders, confirms reservations, coordinates staff, communicates with suppliers. Multiple WhatsApp groups for different purposes. |
| Facebook | Yes — business | Daily | Lunch and evenings | Posts food photos, shares promotions, engages with customer comments. The restaurant's Facebook page is likely the most active marketing channel it currently has. |
| Instagram | Yes — growing | 3–5 times per week | Evenings | Food photography, behind-the-scenes, Stories for daily specials. Follows food bloggers and other Kampala restaurants. |
| TikTok | Occasionally | Casual | Evenings and weekends | Watches food content; considers posting but hasn't started consistently |
| LinkedIn | No | — | — | Not part of current behaviour |
| YouTube | Yes | Several times per week | Late evenings | Cooking techniques, business tips for restaurant owners |
| X / Twitter | Occasionally | — | — | Reads but does not post actively |

**Most reachable on:** Instagram (visual platform matches her product) and Facebook (existing business page audience). WhatsApp for direct engagement once she is warm.

---

**Content She Engages With**

1. **Restaurant business management tips** — "How Kampala restaurant owners are managing staff rosters without spreadsheets" — she is a practitioner, not a reader of theory
2. **Food waste and profit content** — Posts that help her understand how much she is losing to waste, over-portioning, or theft. This is one of her biggest unexplored pain points.
3. **Success stories from similar businesses** — A post by another Kampala bar owner who tried a new POS system and saw specific results. Peer stories from the same sector, same city.
4. **Short practical videos** — How to do something specific in under 60 seconds. She has no patience for long explainers during her working day, but she watches quick useful videos in the evening.
5. **Before/after content about operations** — "Before Maduuka: three WhatsApp groups and a notebook. After: this." The contrast is immediate and felt.

---

**Pain Points**

1. **Peak-hour chaos is impossible to manage properly.** On a Friday night, orders are shouted across a crowded kitchen, cashiers are taking payments without tickets, and by the end of the night the total collected rarely matches the food that was cooked. She knows money is leaking somewhere but cannot locate where.

2. **She does not know which menu items are actually profitable.** She knows her popular dishes. She does not know which ones make her money after ingredient cost and staff time. She has no system for calculating this — she runs on intuition and experience.

3. **Staff management without visibility is exhausting.** Her business depends on people she cannot always see. She needs to trust her staff, but she also needs to know. A cashier who is her cousin may still be pocketing a thousand shillings here and there without a system that catches it.

---

**Goals**

1. Know, by the end of each week, exactly how much money came in, how much was spent on ingredients, and what profit is left — without spending three hours on it.
2. Streamline the order-to-kitchen-to-billing flow so that a Friday night rush does not feel like controlled chaos.
3. Have a system professional enough that she can eventually open a second location without it depending entirely on her physical presence.

---

**Triggers to Follow a Brand**

- An Instagram post appears showing a Kampala restaurant's dashboard or weekly report — she immediately recognises the numbers pattern from her own business
- Another restaurant owner she knows recommends the brand on a WhatsApp group for Kampala hospitality businesses
- The brand posts content that speaks directly to the hospitality sector — not generic business content. The specificity signals that they understand her world.
- She is looking for answers to a specific problem and the brand's content answers it first

---

**Triggers to Unfollow a Brand**

- The brand talks about "restaurants" but clearly means Western fast-casual — not Ugandan hospitality contexts
- Promotional posts that interrupt her feed without giving her anything useful
- Generic business advice that she already knows — tells her to "track expenses" without showing her how to do it in her actual context
- Inconsistent or low-quality visual content — she has a high visual standard from running an Instagram food account and will notice poor design

---

**How Maduuka Fits Her Life**

Amina closes her restaurant around 11pm on a good night. By the time the last table is cleared, she has been on her feet for fourteen hours. She sits down at her desk, opens her cashier's book and the WhatsApp messages from her cook, and tries to reconcile what happened. On most nights, the numbers do not quite add up — and she is too tired to find out why. When Maduuka works for Amina, that 11pm reconciliation takes four minutes on her phone. She sees what was ordered, what was billed, what was paid — split by table, by meal, by cashier. She sees the week's food cost as a percentage of revenue. She knows, for the first time, that her grilled tilapia special is actually her best-margin item and not the nyama choma everyone orders. She still works fourteen hours, but she goes to bed knowing what happened. That is the difference.

---

**Messaging That Resonates**

- **Tone:** Warm, practical, one-business-owner-to-another — she has no patience for lectures but will listen to someone who has clearly been in her kitchen
- **Vocabulary:** Hospitality-specific language — "covers," "table turns," "food cost," "cashier till" — shows genuine sector knowledge; avoid generic retail language
- **Platforms:** Instagram (food photos and Stories are her primary digital presence); Facebook (existing community); WhatsApp (direct conversion)
- **Content style:** Visual-forward; short sharp captions; real Kampala restaurant context; before/after format works well
- **Proof type:** Testimonials from other Ugandan hospitality businesses with specific metrics ("I used to lose UGX X per week in untracked sales"); video testimonials from someone who looks and sounds like her

---

**Messaging to Avoid**

- Retail-specific language ("shelf stock," "POS terminal for shops") without hospitality equivalents — she needs to see her business reflected
- "We integrate with your accounting software" — she does not have accounting software; that framing assumes a level of digital infrastructure she does not have
- Complex dashboard screenshots without context — they look impressive but do not tell her what changed in her daily life

---

**Assumptions Flag**

> *Assumption: Income level estimated from typical Kampala mid-range restaurant norms. The waitlist contact is a bar — confirm whether bar economics and pain points differ meaningfully from restaurant pain points for persona validation.*
> *Assumption: Instagram and Facebook presence assumed based on typical Kampala hospitality sector digital behaviour; first conversation with waitlist contact will validate.*

## Persona 3: Dr. Joseph Ssemukasa — "The Professional Pharmacist"

**Archetype:** The credential-holder who needs his systems to match his professional standards

---

**Demographics**

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Name | Dr. Joseph Ssemukasa |
| Age | 36–52 |
| Gender | Male (pharmacy ownership in Uganda skews male among degree-holders; female pharmacy owners are common and the persona applies equally — content should not be gender-exclusionary) |
| Location | Kampala — Wandegeya, Mulago, Kikuubo, or suburban commercial area near a hospital or clinic; runs a private community pharmacy |
| Income level | UGX 5M–12M per month business revenue; personal income after expenses: UGX 2M–4M — *Assumption: based on typical Kampala private pharmacy economics* |
| Education | Bachelor of Pharmacy (Makerere or Mbarara University); registered with the Pharmacy Board of Uganda |
| Occupation | Owner and dispensing pharmacist; employs 1–3 staff (dispensary assistant, cashier) |
| Household | Married, children; owns or buying property; considers expanding to a second pharmacy branch within 3–5 years |

---

**Platform Usage**

| Platform | Uses it? | Frequency | Peak time | How they use it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| WhatsApp | Yes — essential | All day | Operating hours | Receives prescriptions by photo from doctors and clinics, supplier communication, patient follow-ups, professional pharmacy groups |
| LinkedIn | Yes — periodic | 2–4 times per week | Mornings before work, evenings | Follows pharmaceutical industry news, NDA updates, professional associations. Posts occasionally. Far more LinkedIn-active than Patrick or Amina. |
| Facebook | Yes | Several times per week | Evenings | Health information pages, professional groups, community pharmacy Facebook pages |
| YouTube | Yes | Weekly | Evenings and weekends | Pharmaceutical training content, business management videos, NDA and EFRIS compliance guidance |
| Instagram | Occasionally | 1–2 times per week | Evenings | Health awareness content; not a primary platform |
| X / Twitter | Occasionally | — | — | Follows NDA and URA official accounts; reads regulatory news |
| TikTok | No / rarely | — | — | Not part of his digital behaviour |

**Most reachable on:** LinkedIn (professional content, regulatory topics) and WhatsApp (direct professional communication). Facebook and YouTube for secondary reach.

---

**Content He Engages With**

1. **Regulatory compliance content** — EFRIS requirements for pharmacies, NDA inspection preparation, tax compliance for healthcare businesses. This is his primary anxiety and he searches for it actively.
2. **Drug expiry and inventory management content** — Posts that address the specific pharmacist problem of first-expiry-first-out stock management, cold chain compliance, controlled substance record-keeping. Generic inventory content does not resonate; pharmacy-specific does.
3. **Professional peer content** — Other Ugandan pharmacists sharing business management experiences; Uganda Pharmacy Association updates; case studies from pharmacies that improved their operations.
4. **Data and numbers content** — He is quantitatively minded. Posts with statistics, charts, and measurable outcomes engage him. "Our pharmacy reduced drug expiry losses by 40% in 3 months" is a headline he reads carefully.
5. **Technology adoption content** — He is interested in digital tools for his profession. He reads about health tech and pharmacy management software. He is more willing than Patrick or Amina to engage with product demonstration content.

---

**Pain Points**

1. **Drug expiry is a direct financial and legal risk.** Every expired drug on the shelf is a loss: the procurement cost is written off, but the liability remains. NDA inspectors check expiry dates. A pharmacist with a reputation for expired stock is in professional trouble, not just financial trouble.

2. **Multi-system compliance is manual and fragile.** He must comply with EFRIS (URA tax receipting), NDA regulations (drug register, dispensing records), and now potentially digital record-keeping requirements. He is managing these across different tools — or a dangerous mix of paper and spreadsheet — and he knows this will not hold up to an inspection or a business expansion.

3. **He cannot open a second branch without better systems.** He has the professional credentials and the ambition to expand. What he does not have is a system that could run a second location without him being physically present. His current setup is as dependent on him as a body is dependent on a spine.

---

**Goals**

1. Full, defensible compliance with NDA and EFRIS requirements — systems that produce records he can show an inspector without scrambling.
2. Reduce drug expiry losses to near zero through better first-expiry-first-out tracking and automated low-stock alerts.
3. Build the operational foundation for a second pharmacy branch in the next two to three years — systems that can scale without him.

---

**Triggers to Follow a Brand**

- A LinkedIn post about pharmacy management in Uganda, EFRIS requirements for healthcare businesses, or NDA compliance appears in his feed. He clicks because it is exactly what he has been searching for.
- A trusted colleague — another pharmacist, a pharmacy association figure — recommends the brand.
- The content demonstrates genuine knowledge of pharmacy-specific requirements, not generic inventory management. Specificity earns trust.
- The brand has verifiable credentials — a real company, real people, a working demo, a real website. He will check before trusting.

---

**Triggers to Unfollow a Brand**

- Content that conflates pharmacy stock management with general retail stock management — a drug dispensary is not a supermarket and he knows the difference
- Over-promising without evidence — "Africa's leading pharmacy management system" without any substantiation
- Infrequent posting — he follows professional accounts expecting regular, credible updates; long silences read as either instability or irrelevance
- American or European regulatory references without Uganda/EA equivalents — content that does not acknowledge the actual regulatory environment he works in

---

**How Maduuka Fits His Life**

Every month, Dr. Joseph does a manual stock count that takes most of a Sunday. He goes aisle by aisle, checking expiry dates, writing quantities in a ledger, then transferring numbers to a spreadsheet that was built by a nephew three years ago and no longer quite works correctly. He has found expired paracetamol twice this year. Both times it cost him money and a conversation with his dispenser that he did not enjoy having. When Maduuka works for Dr. Joseph, the Sunday stock count becomes a twenty-minute phone review. He sees every product with its expiry date, its current quantity, and a flag if anything is within sixty days of expiry. He sees what needs to be reordered before he runs out. He sees the month's EFRIS receipts already reconciled. He goes to church on Sunday with his family instead of counting tablets. That is the version of his professional life that Maduuka is promising — and it needs to be specific enough that he can see it.

---

**Messaging That Resonates**

- **Tone:** Professional, peer-level, evidence-led — he respects credentials and expertise; casual tone feels off-brand for his professional identity
- **Vocabulary:** Pharmaceutical and regulatory language used correctly: "dispensing records," "first-expiry-first-out," "controlled substances register," "NDA compliance," "cold chain" — this signals genuine understanding
- **Platforms:** LinkedIn (primary for professional content); WhatsApp (direct conversion after trust is established); Facebook and YouTube for educational content
- **Content style:** Data-led, specific, professional design — not casual or entertainment-first; a short well-sourced stat outperforms a meme
- **Proof type:** Pharmacy-specific case studies with named outcomes; endorsements from pharmacy professional associations or recognisable individual pharmacists; video walkthroughs showing actual pharmacy compliance features

---

**Messaging to Avoid**

- Generic retail language without pharmacy-specific adaptation
- "Easy to use" without showing how specifically it handles the complexity of a pharmacy (expiry tracking, controlled substance logs, NDA reporting)
- Social media content that is too casual for his professional self-image — he posts professionally on LinkedIn; Maduuka's tone there must match his register
- Pricing comparisons to Western pharmacy software (irrelevant to his context)

---

**Assumptions Flag**

> *Assumption: Income level estimated from typical Kampala private community pharmacy norms; validate with real data from first pharmacy clients.*
> *Assumption: NDA compliance features assumed as relevant; confirm with product team which specific NDA requirements Maduuka currently covers and which are on the roadmap — do not claim compliance features that do not exist.*
> *Assumption: LinkedIn activity assumed based on education level and professional registration; validate in first conversations with pharmacy prospects.*

**Strategic Note**

Patrick is the highest-volume opportunity and should receive the most content investment in Phase 1. There are significantly more retail shop owners in Kampala than pharmacies or restaurants, and the pirated desktop POS replacement angle gives Maduuka a large addressable market it can reach without competing directly on features against global brands. The EFRIS anxiety is an immediate, live pain point that creates urgency — content addressing it will outperform generic product promotion.

Amina is the highest-priority individual lead in the short term because she is already on the waitlist. Close her as a paying client before the first post goes live. A hospitality case study from week one changes everything.

Dr. Joseph represents a higher lifetime value per client and a reputationally valuable early adopter. A pharmacy as a reference client creates credibility with other professional sector buyers (clinics, schools, NGOs) far beyond the pharmacy vertical. However, his compliance requirements demand that Maduuka's feature claims are accurate — do not overpromise NDA integration features that are not yet built.

**Platform overlap:** Patrick and Amina both use Facebook actively. Facebook is the one platform where a single piece of content — a well-targeted boosted post or a strong organic post in an SME Facebook group — can reach both personas simultaneously. Early paid spend on Facebook should target both segments with a single message about EFRIS compliance and stock visibility, which resonates across retail and hospitality.
