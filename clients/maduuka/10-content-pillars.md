# Maduuka — Content Pillars
**Skill:** 10-content-pillars
**Date:** March 2026
**Pillars:** 3 (small business posting 3–5 times per week, pre-revenue phase)
**Prepared by:** Peter Bamuhigire / Chwezi Core Systems

*Input: 01-client-brief, 03-audience-personas, 04-brand-voice-intake. Platforms in scope: LinkedIn, Instagram, Facebook, X/Twitter. Brand tone: Sharp, Grounded, Empowering. 90-day goal: 20 paying clients.*

---

## Pillar Recommendation

Three pillars are recommended. Maduuka is a new brand with no existing audience, a small team, and a 90-day client acquisition focus. Three well-maintained pillars are more effective than five under-served ones. As the business grows and content capacity increases, a fourth pillar (client community and retention) should be added at the Phase 2 review.

Apply the 10-4-1 rule (Bodnar and Cohen, 2012) across the full content mix: for every 15 posts, 10 are value-led with no direct product agenda, 4 are original Maduuka brand content, and 1 is a direct promotional post with a CTA.

Apply the 8 Imprints Rule (Pinskey, 1997): a business owner needs to encounter Maduuka approximately 8 times before they will take action. Three posts per week means a prospect who follows on day one will cross the 8-imprint threshold within 19 days. This is the minimum frequency to make conversion likely within the 90-day window.

## Pillar 1: The Business Owner's Edge

### 1. Pillar Name
**The Business Owner's Edge**

### 2. Purpose Statement
This pillar exists to make Maduuka the most useful brand in an SME owner's feed by providing practical, specific knowledge about running a business in Uganda — building the trust and credibility that turns a follower into a buyer.

### 3. Hero / Hub / Hygiene Tier Assignment
**Dominant tier: Hub** — content in this pillar appears on a regular, expected schedule (weekly business insight, EFRIS explainer series, compliance guidance). Audiences follow Maduuka for this content specifically; consistency is the value. Secondary tier: Hygiene — FAQ-style posts (What is EFRIS? What records does a shop need to keep?) are always-on search-driven content that drives organic reach.

### 4. Example Post Types

1. A five-slide Instagram carousel titled "EFRIS in plain English: what every Kampala shop owner needs to know before the inspector arrives" — one concept per slide, no jargon, specific URA requirements named
2. A LinkedIn text post from Peter's personal account: "I spent three years watching Ugandan shop owners lose 15–20% of their stock to untracked sales. Here is the specific thing that makes this happen — and what stops it." [AIDA structure; real-world specific]
3. A Facebook post: "Running a restaurant or bar in Kampala? Here is the one number you need to check every single week to know if you are making money." [hook into food cost as a percentage of revenue]
4. An Instagram Reel (60 seconds): "Three records every business in Uganda must keep for URA — filmed at a real Kampala shop" [educational; practical; not promotional]
5. A LinkedIn poll from the Maduuka Company Page: "How does your business currently handle EFRIS receipts?" Options: "We have a system / We do it manually / We are not sure if we are compliant / We didn't know it was required." [High engagement; audience data; follow-up post with results]

### 5. Percentage of Content Mix
**40%** of total posts.

This pillar carries 40% because the primary 90-day goal is client acquisition, and client acquisition in a market with no existing brand awareness requires trust-building before selling. The Business Owner's Edge is the trust engine. A business owner who has found three Maduuka posts genuinely useful will give the brand the benefit of the doubt when it asks for a demo. The 10-4-1 ratio means 10 of every 15 posts are value-first — this pillar carries the majority of that 10.

### 6. Platforms It Suits Best

**LinkedIn** — The professional audience on LinkedIn searches for business guidance, regulatory information, and sector insight. EFRIS and compliance content is actively searched here by business managers and owners. Peter's personal account amplifies this content directly to his professional network.

**Instagram** — Educational carousels and short Reels perform well for the urban 28–40 audience. The visual format of a carousel (one tip per slide, strong cover image) is Instagram-native and highly shareable. Amina (restaurant owner) is most reachable here.

**Facebook** — EFRIS and business management content shared in Ugandan SME Facebook groups gets organic reach the brand cannot generate from a new page alone. Post in relevant groups (with permission) as the content builds.

### 7. Ten Starter Content Ideas

1. **Instagram carousel** — "EFRIS in plain English: 5 things every Kampala shop owner needs to know." Covers: what EFRIS is, who it applies to, what you need to set up, what the penalty is for non-compliance, and one action to take this week. *Intended action: save the post; DM for help.*

2. **LinkedIn text post (Peter personal)** — "I used to think the biggest threat to a small shop was theft. After building Maduuka, I changed my mind. Here's what actually drains the money." [Reveals: untracked sales, not theft, is the primary loss vector.] *Intended feeling: recognition; curiosity about the solution.*

3. **Facebook post** — "What does a good week look like for a restaurant in Kampala? This one number tells you everything." [Food cost as a percentage of revenue: if it's above 35%, the week was not good regardless of how busy it was.] *Intended action: comment with their own experience; share to fellow restaurant owners.*

4. **Instagram Reel (60 seconds)** — "Three records every Uganda business must keep for URA." Filmed as a talking-head with text overlays. Specific legal requirements named. No product mention. *Intended feeling: informed and grateful.*

5. **LinkedIn document carousel (Maduuka Company Page)** — "The 10-point EFRIS compliance checklist for retail businesses in Uganda." One requirement per slide. Final slide: "Download this checklist — link in first comment." *Intended action: follow the page; click the link.*

6. **X/Twitter thread (Peter personal)** — "Most Kampala business owners are running EFRIS on a manual system that will not hold up to an audit. Thread: what you need, what you don't, and what happens if you get it wrong." [5-tweet thread with specific URA guidance.] *Intended action: retweet; DM with questions.*

7. **Facebook post** — "If you have a shop and you use a cashier, read this." [Explains the three most common ways stock disappears through cashier gaps — not accusatory, systemic.] *Intended feeling: recognition; desire for a system that closes these gaps.*

8. **Instagram carousel** — "Running a pharmacy in Uganda? These are the records NDA inspectors check first." [4 slides: drug register, dispensing log, EFRIS receipts, cold chain record. Practical and specific.] *Intended action: save; DM for advice.*

9. **LinkedIn text post (Maduuka Company Page)** — "The difference between a business that grows and one that stays the same is usually not effort. It is information. Here is the one report every SME owner should generate every Monday morning." [Explains week-over-week sales comparison.] *Intended feeling: aspiration to be the business owner who knows.*

10. **Facebook post** — "Every business owner in Uganda needs to understand EFRIS before the end of this year. Here is a simple guide — share with anyone who needs it." [Short plain-English explainer. Share-friendly.] *Intended action: share to business networks; page likes from shares.*

### 8. What NOT to Post

- Do not turn this pillar into a sales channel — The Business Owner's Edge is for giving, not asking. No pricing, no CTAs to book a demo, no "Maduuka solves this." The product is implied by the knowledge; let the audience draw the connection.
- Do not share content that has not been verified against current URA and NDA regulations — incorrect compliance guidance damages the brand irreparably. Every regulatory claim should be checked before publishing.
- Do not use generic business advice that applies equally to a business in London or Lagos — this pillar's value is its Kampala/Uganda specificity. "Track your expenses" is not useful here. "Track your EFRIS receipts against your sales total every Friday" is.
- Do not publish this content without a consistent posting schedule — the pillar's value compounds over time through trust. One good post, then silence for two weeks, does the opposite. Hub content requires rhythm.

## Pillar 2: Real Business Stories

### 1. Pillar Name
**Real Business Stories**

### 2. Purpose Statement
This pillar exists to show what Maduuka looks like in actual use by real Ugandan businesses — converting scepticism into belief by replacing abstract claims with specific, named outcomes from real people in recognisable situations.

### 3. Hero / Hub / Hygiene Tier Assignment
**Dominant tier: Hub** — regular client spotlights, milestone posts, and community content appear on a weekly or fortnightly schedule and build community over time. Secondary tier: Hero — a major case study (the first paying client's full story, or a multi-business comparison piece) is a planned high-impact post that can be boosted and repurposed across platforms.

### 4. Example Post Types

1. A LinkedIn text post: "Amina opened her restaurant in 2021. For two years, every Friday night ended the same way — a cashier's book that did not add up and no idea why. Three weeks ago she signed up for Maduuka. Here is what changed." [Full Challenge/Approach/Result narrative; with permission.]
2. An Instagram Reel (60–90 seconds): A real client — Patrick, a Kampala retailer — on camera for 30 seconds explaining what his stock count used to take and what it takes now. No script; natural voice; subtitles. Filmed at his actual shop.
3. A Facebook post: Before and after — a phone photo of a handwritten stock ledger (before) and a screenshot of a Maduuka weekly report (after). Caption: "Same shop. Same products. This is what three weeks changes." [With client permission.]
4. An Instagram carousel: "A Kampala pharmacist's EFRIS story — before and after." Five slides covering the situation before, the problem with the old system, what was set up, the first inspection after Maduuka, and the result.
5. A LinkedIn document carousel (Maduuka Company Page): "Three Kampala businesses. Three different problems. One dashboard." A multi-business case study covering retail, hospitality, and pharmacy — showing that the same platform handles different sectors.

### 5. Percentage of Content Mix
**30%** of total posts.

This pillar carries 30% because social proof is the most powerful conversion mechanism in the EA market (Bodnar and Cohen, 2012 note the primacy of peer endorsement over advertising). In a market where trust is built through community, a testimonial from a Kampala business owner is worth more than any product claim Maduuka can make for itself. However, this pillar depends entirely on having real clients — it cannot be published authentically until paying clients exist. In Month 1, this pillar launches with the first paying client. Until then, the content calendar leans heavier on Pillar 1.

### 6. Platforms It Suits Best

**Instagram** — Visual before/after posts, short client Reels, and genuine behind-the-scenes content perform strongly here. The visual format of an Instagram carousel telling a business story — real person, real shop, real result — creates social proof in the most shareable format available on any platform.

**Facebook** — Business owner stories are shared heavily in Ugandan SME Facebook groups. A compelling case study that resonates will travel organically through shares and group posts in a way that no other content type achieves. Facebook is the word-of-mouth amplifier for this pillar.

**LinkedIn** — Professional case studies (Dr. Joseph's pharmacy story; a multi-sector comparison piece) reach the decision-maker audience that needs peer validation before purchasing. LinkedIn's native document carousel format is ideal for a multi-slide business story.

### 7. Ten Starter Content Ideas

1. **Instagram Reel** — First client story: 60-second video of Amina (or Patrick, or Dr. Joseph — whoever signs up first) at their business, in their own words, answering: "What was the problem before? What is different now?" Raw, real, subtitled. *Intended feeling: recognition and trust.*

2. **Facebook post** — "Kampala shop owner: how we went from a Sunday stock count that took all day to a 20-minute Monday morning check." Before/after narrative post with the client's permission and real numbers. *Intended action: share with other shop owners.*

3. **LinkedIn text post (Peter personal)** — "I watched Patrick do his stock count for the first time after signing up for Maduuka. Here is what happened." [Personal founder narrative, not a product pitch — a real story told firsthand.] *Intended feeling: the founder genuinely cares about the client's outcome.*

4. **Instagram carousel** — "What a Kampala restaurant's weekly report looks like — and what the numbers mean." Real dashboard data (anonymised if needed). Slide 1: total sales. Slide 2: food cost percentage. Slide 3: the one item that made the most profit this week. Slide 4: what this tells the owner. *Intended action: save; DM for a demo.*

5. **LinkedIn document carousel (Maduuka Company Page)** — "From pirated desktop to cloud: one Kampala retailer's 30-day transition." A full case study covering the before situation, the migration, the first month of data, and the specific outcome. *Intended action: follow the page; reach out.*

6. **X/Twitter post** — "A pharmacist in Kampala reduced drug expiry losses by [X]% in 60 days. The change was not about effort. It was about information. Thread." [Links to LinkedIn article or Instagram carousel with the full story.] *Intended action: click through to the full story.*

7. **Facebook post** — "We asked a Kampala business owner what they wished they had known when they started. This is what they said." [Client-sourced quote about the value of proper records early in a business, not about Maduuka specifically.] *Intended feeling: resonance; Maduuka associated with business wisdom.*

8. **Instagram Reel** — Behind the scenes: Peter visits Amina's restaurant for a monthly review. 60 seconds of natural footage — the report open on a phone, conversation about the numbers, the owner's reaction. No script. *Intended feeling: this is a real relationship, not a software transaction.*

9. **LinkedIn text post (Maduuka Company Page)** — "One year ago this business was managing stock with three WhatsApp groups and a notebook. Here is where they are now." [12-month anniversary post once a client has been on the platform that long — plan this in advance.] *Intended feeling: long-term commitment.*

10. **Facebook/Instagram** — "Meet [Client Name] — [their business description] in [location], Kampala." A one-paragraph profile of a client with a photo of them at their business. No sales angle — just celebrating the business owner as a person. *Intended feeling: community; this brand cares about its clients as people.*

### 8. What NOT to Post

- Do not post about a client without their explicit permission in writing (or voice note) — even if the story is positive. Data about their business is theirs, not Maduuka's.
- Do not fabricate or embellish client results — specific numbers must come directly from the client. If a client does not want to share exact numbers, use percentage improvements or qualitative descriptions rather than invented specifics.
- Do not post this content before the first paying client exists — placeholder "future client" stories or fictional scenarios destroy trust. This pillar launches when real clients exist. Until then, use Pillar 1 content to fill the calendar.
- Do not make the product the hero of client stories — the client is the protagonist; Maduuka is the tool. "Maduuka transformed this business" is wrong framing. "This business owner built a system that works — here is how" is right.
- Do not post case studies without a brief explanation of the starting context — a win means nothing without understanding the challenge it represents.

## Pillar 3: Inside Maduuka

### 1. Pillar Name
**Inside Maduuka**

### 2. Purpose Statement
This pillar exists to make the product credible and the company human — giving potential clients a clear picture of what Maduuka does, who builds it, and why — so that by the time they book a demo, they are not encountering a stranger.

### 3. Hero / Hub / Hygiene Tier Assignment
**Dominant tier: Hygiene** — product explainers, feature walkthroughs, FAQs, and pricing posts are always-on search-driven content that answers the questions potential clients are actively asking before they commit to a demo. Secondary tier: Hero — product launch announcements, the iOS launch campaign, and major feature releases are high-effort, high-reach Hero content planned well in advance.

### 4. Example Post Types

1. An Instagram Reel (45 seconds): "How Maduuka handles EFRIS — watched in real time." A screen recording of the EFRIS receipt workflow on an Android phone, overlaid with a narration. Clean, specific, no fluff.
2. A LinkedIn text post (Peter personal): "Why we built Maduuka for Android first — and what that decision cost us." [Honest product development insight; positions the product as built for the market's reality.]
3. An Instagram carousel: "Maduuka in 5 slides: what it does, what it costs, and how to start." [Product overview; the clearest possible summary of the value proposition. Updated whenever pricing or features change.]
4. A Facebook post: "Every business that signs up for Maduuka gets a personal onboarding call. Here is what we cover in the first 20 minutes." [Demystifies the onboarding process; reduces the friction of signing up.]
5. A LinkedIn document carousel (Maduuka Company Page): "Here is the exact data a Kampala pharmacy can see on Maduuka — and what each number means for the business." [Real dashboard walkthrough; pharmacy-specific.]

### 5. Percentage of Content Mix
**30%** of total posts.

This pillar carries 30% because without product clarity, interest generated by Pillars 1 and 2 cannot convert. A potential client who finds the EFRIS guides useful will ask: "What do these people actually do?" Inside Maduuka answers that question directly. However, it does not exceed 30% because too much product content without surrounding value and social proof reads as advertising — it will cause unfollows and undermine the trust built by Pillars 1 and 2.

### 6. Platforms It Suits Best

**Instagram** — Screen-recording Reels of the product in action are the most efficient demonstration format on Instagram. The feature set becomes tangible when the audience can see the interface doing what it claims to do. Stories are ideal for quick product updates, new feature announcements, and "did you know Maduuka can do this?" moments.

**LinkedIn** — Product explainer carousels and technical posts perform well for the Dr. Joseph (pharmacist) audience and any professional buyer who wants to understand the product before booking a demo. LinkedIn is where the software-buyer decision-maker audience is most receptive to feature and capability content.

### 7. Ten Starter Content Ideas

1. **Instagram Reel** — "Maduuka in 60 seconds: stock in, stock out, report ready." A screen recording of the core workflow from adding a new product to generating a weekly stock report. No voiceover needed; text overlays suffice. *Intended action: save; visit website.*

2. **LinkedIn text post (Peter personal)** — "We built Maduuka to work on a UGX 450,000 Android smartphone with slow internet. Here is why that was the most important technical decision we made." [Founder insight that positions the product's design decisions as empathetic to the market.] *Intended feeling: the people who built this understand our reality.*

3. **Instagram carousel** — "What Maduuka costs, what it includes, and how to start." Slide 1: pricing. Slide 2: what is included. Slide 3: the free trial terms. Slide 4: how to sign up. Slide 5: the CTA. *Intended action: visit the website; start a trial.*

4. **Facebook post** — "How does Maduuka handle EFRIS? Here is the 3-minute answer." [A short, specific explanation of the EFRIS integration — what happens when a sale is made, how the receipt is generated, where the data goes. Factual.] *Intended action: DM with specific questions.*

5. **Instagram Reel** — "The Maduuka closing-time report: what a restaurant owner sees at 11pm." A screen recording of the daily sales summary dashboard, narrated in the restaurant context. Specific: total covers, total revenue, average spend per table. *Intended action: book a demo.*

6. **LinkedIn document carousel (Maduuka Company Page)** — "Maduuka for pharmacies: the 6 records it manages automatically." Each slide covers one record type: drug register, EFRIS receipts, expiry tracking, controlled substances, supplier invoices, daily sales. *Intended action: follow the page; request a pharmacy-specific demo.*

7. **X/Twitter** — "Maduuka is now live on Android and web. Free trial: [link]. If you run a shop, restaurant, or pharmacy in Uganda and you are still doing this manually — try it." *Intended action: visit the website.*

8. **Instagram Story series** — "Did you know Maduuka can do this?" — A weekly Story series (every Friday) featuring one feature or use case. Each Story is one slide: a screen recording and a single sentence explaining what it does. Archived as a Highlight titled "What Maduuka Does." *Intended action: saves as reference; returns to watch the series.*

9. **LinkedIn text post (Maduuka Company Page)** — "Here is how we onboard a new client. It takes about two hours the first time. Here is what happens in each step." [Transparent, demystifying — reducing the friction of the unknown that stops people booking a demo.] *Intended feeling: this is manageable; I know what to expect.*

10. **Facebook/Instagram post** — "Maduuka is coming to iPhone. We are not ready to share a date yet. Here is why it is taking time — and what we are building." [Soft iOS anticipation post — honest about the timeline, shows product investment.] *Intended feeling: this team is serious about building something lasting.*

### 8. What NOT to Post

- Do not claim features that are not yet built or not yet available on the current release — this includes any NDA integration or iOS availability claims that have not been confirmed with the product team
- Do not post product content without checking it is current — a screenshot of a UI that has since changed creates confusion and undermines trust
- Do not turn this pillar into a catalogue — listing every feature in every post makes the product feel overwhelming and the content feel like documentation, not brand communication
- Do not post promotional product content more than once per week — per the 10-4-1 ratio, one post in every 15 should be directly promotional; exceeding this rate will cause unfollows and reduce the reach of the value-led content
- Do not post product screenshots without context — a dashboard screenshot without a caption explaining what the numbers mean and why they matter is noise, not content

> **Consultant note — 8 Imprints in practice for Maduuka:** At three posts per week on LinkedIn and Instagram, a Kampala business owner who follows Maduuka on day one will hit 8 imprints within 19 days — just crossing the threshold for action-readiness. Every week of consistent posting compounds. Every week of silence loses ground. For a 90-day client acquisition target, the posting schedule is not a content marketing preference — it is the mechanism of conversion. Three posts per week across LinkedIn and Instagram is the minimum. Any week with fewer than three posts should be treated as a miss, not a skip.
>
> **The 10-4-1 rule in East African context:** In Uganda, audiences are more sceptical of promotional content than in Western markets, and peer trust is proportionally more valuable. Skewing toward the "10" (educational and value-led content) builds trust faster here than anywhere. The "1" promotional post per 15 posts is not a constraint — it is a protection against burning trust for short-term clicks.
