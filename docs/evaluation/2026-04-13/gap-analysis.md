# Gap Analysis

## Primary Conclusion

The repository's biggest limitation is not coverage. It is adaptability. Social Media Skills already covers most of the workflow needed to run a serious consultancy. What prevents it from operating confidently across global markets is that country, culture, platform, pricing, and regulatory differences are not yet encoded as a first-class system.

## Gap 1: No Market Adaptation Engine

There is no mandatory upstream mechanism that says, before generating any strategy or deliverable:

- what country or region this is for,
- what the relevant platform realities are,
- what legal constraints apply,
- what price sensitivity looks like,
- what tone norms and cultural risks exist,
- what trust and conversion behaviours are normal in that market.

As a result, many downstream skills inherit East Africa defaults unless the operator overrides them manually.

## Gap 2: EA Defaults Are Stronger Than Global Override Logic

The repository's defaults are clear and useful for Uganda and East Africa, but global override logic is weak. This affects:

- UGX-centred pricing assumptions.
- WhatsApp-first communication paths.
- Channel priorities that may not hold in Europe, North America, East Asia, or the Gulf.
- Audience and trust assumptions shaped by EA digital behaviour.

The issue is not having a regional default. The issue is failing to turn that default into a configurable context layer.

## Gap 3: Regulatory Coverage Is Partial And Uneven

Some skills show solid awareness of GDPR, CCPA, UK/US/EU copyright differences, and East African privacy law. That is encouraging. But regulatory awareness is not systematised across the repository.

Missing or underdeveloped areas include:

- country-by-country marketing compliance routing,
- consent and tracking implications by market,
- sector-specific legal sensitivity such as health, finance, and political communications,
- localisation of retention and data-use guidance,
- decision rules for when legal review becomes mandatory.

## Gap 4: Cultural Calibration Is Too Thin

The repository has strong language and voice standards, but there is not yet a full cross-cultural calibration layer. A global consultancy engine should help the operator adjust for:

- direct versus indirect communication norms,
- humour and irony tolerance,
- prestige and status signalling,
- individualist versus collectivist purchase motivations,
- local trust signals and proof mechanisms,
- imagery, symbolism, and taboo sensitivity.

At present, these adjustments depend too heavily on the consultant's own experience.

## Gap 5: Global Platform Usage Logic Is Incomplete

Platform-specific skills are strong, but market-specific platform relevance is not mapped systematically. A world-class engine should know that:

- WhatsApp may be dominant in one market and secondary in another.
- X/Twitter can be politically relevant in some countries and commercially marginal in others.
- LinkedIn maturity varies heavily by sector and geography.
- Search, email, messaging apps, creator platforms, and local networks all vary in importance by market.

Without a market mapping layer, platform strategy risks being directionally correct but locally suboptimal.

## Gap 6: Pricing And Budget Logic Are Not Fully Localised

The commercial side of the repository is credible, but pricing and budget guidance remain heavily EA-oriented. To work globally, the system needs:

- purchasing-power-aware pricing logic,
- agency pricing models by market maturity,
- retainer versus project packaging norms by region,
- expectations around procurement, invoicing, taxes, and enterprise buying cycles.

Without that, the system can write proposals globally but cannot always price or package them credibly.

## Gap 7: Execution Boundaries Limit Full-Service Delivery

This gap is intentional, but it still matters for evaluation. The repository does not handle:

- graphic design production,
- video production/editing,
- website implementation,
- live ad account management and optimisation,
- legal contracting and negotiation.

That means it is better understood as a consultancy operating system than an all-in-one agency production stack.

## Gap 8: Some Enterprise And Cross-Functional Depth Is Missing

The system is strong for boutique agency use, but a world-class global engine would need more built-in support for:

- procurement-heavy enterprise sales,
- internal stakeholder mapping,
- board and CFO-level business cases,
- cross-functional marketing-sales-product alignment,
- multinational governance across regions and brands.

Some of this is partially present, but not deeply enough to claim enterprise-grade global readiness.

## What Most Directly Prevents Global Operation

If one issue had to be named as the blocker, it would be this:

**The repository lacks a universal context adaptation layer that all major skills must consume before generating strategy, plans, pricing, and reporting.**

Until that is added, the system can operate globally only through expert manual interpretation rather than through embedded design.
