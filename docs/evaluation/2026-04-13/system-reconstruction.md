# System Reconstruction

## How The Repository Functions As A Consultancy System

This repository is not organised as a single master workflow file. Instead, it functions as a modular operating system built from many specialised skills. The lifecycle is reconstructed by following the naming scheme, repository guidance in [README.md](/C:/wamp64/www/social-media-skills/README.md:1), behavioural rules in [CLAUDE.md](/C:/wamp64/www/social-media-skills/CLAUDE.md:1), and the internal logic of the major `SKILL.md` files.

At a system level, the repository behaves like this:

1. Acquire and position the consultancy.
2. Capture client context and audit the current state.
3. Produce strategic recommendations.
4. Translate strategy into channel plans, campaign plans, calendars, and operating playbooks.
5. Measure outcomes and justify investment.
6. Retain, upsell, and operationalise delivery over time.

## Business Development

The business development layer is robust. Skills such as `biz-dev-practitioner-positioning`, `biz-dev-credentials`, `biz-dev-proposal`, `biz-dev-pricing-menu`, `biz-dev-video-outreach`, and `biz-dev-social-media-audit-offer` cover the front end of consultancy growth.

Real-world interpretation:

- The consultant can define a niche and position the practice.
- The consultant can package the offer, document social proof, and present credentials.
- The consultant can run outbound prospecting through audit offers and personalised outreach.
- The consultant can turn interest into a structured proposal with scope, fees, and deliverables.

This is a meaningful differentiator because many so-called skill repositories start at content generation and ignore how an agency actually wins work.

## Onboarding

The onboarding layer is structured and commercially realistic. `01-client-brief`, `02-platform-audit`, `03-audience-personas`, and `04-brand-voice-intake` create a coherent intake sequence.

The flow is roughly:

1. Capture business context, objectives, products, offers, geography, and constraints.
2. Audit existing channels and benchmark current performance.
3. Build audience personas to guide targeting and messaging.
4. Define brand voice, language boundaries, and messaging style.

This means the strategy layer is not being generated in a vacuum. That is a consultancy-grade operating model.

## Strategy

The strategy layer is one of the strongest parts of the system. It is split across foundational strategy skills (`05-social-media-strategy`, `06-digital-marketing-strategy`, `07-email-marketing-strategy`, `08-influencer-marketing-strategy`, `09-campaign-strategy`) and a deeper framework layer (`peso-integrated-strategy`, `owned-media-strategy`, `strategy-channel-architecture`, `strategy-customer-value-journey`, `strategy-organic-paid-hybrid`, `strategy-pdca-workflow-design`, and others).

This creates a strong strategic stack:

- Business goals can be translated into channel roles.
- Content, funnel stages, and customer journey logic can be aligned.
- Organic, paid, owned, and earned activity can be coordinated.
- Specific use cases such as personal brand, social commerce, or micro-communities are covered.

The key observation is that the repository does not just ask for “a strategy”; it gives the operator multiple strategy abstractions to combine depending on the client situation.

## Planning

Planning skills convert strategy into an executable shape. `10-content-pillars`, `11-content-calendar`, `12-website-content-plan`, `13-campaign-brief` are the bridge between strategic recommendation and ongoing delivery.

In a live consultancy context, these skills would be used to:

- Define the recurring themes that keep channel messaging coherent.
- Map content to dates, objectives, and platforms.
- Specify campaign creative intent and key messages.
- Align website or landing-page content with the social and digital funnel.

This is where the system stops being theoretical and becomes operational.

## Platform-Specific Design

The platform layer is comprehensive. It includes Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, WhatsApp, TikTok, YouTube, X/Twitter, Google Business Profile, podcasting, and more.

This matters because a real consultancy does not deliver a generic strategy; it adapts to platform mechanics. The platform skills appear designed to handle:

- Platform role in the mix.
- Content formats and rhythms.
- Audience expectations by platform.
- Community or sales behaviour.
- Integration with broader goals such as lead generation, discovery, education, or authority building.

The strongest pattern here is that platform plans sit downstream of strategy rather than replacing it.

## Execution Playbooks

The playbook layer is broad enough to resemble an agency operations manual. Skills cover community management, crisis communications, content production, governance, customer service, WhatsApp and SMS operations, reputation management, employee advocacy, lead magnets, email funnels, social selling, white-label partnerships, retainer management, and more.

These playbooks reconstruct the day-to-day engine of delivery:

- How the team responds to audience interactions.
- How approvals, escalation, and governance work.
- How content gets briefed and quality controlled.
- How leads move from social to DM to WhatsApp to sales conversation.
- How the agency manages scope, renewals, and client communications.

This is one of the clearest signs that the repository is meant to run a consultancy, not merely write captions.

## Reporting And Optimisation

The analytics layer is more serious than most agency templates. `meta-reporting`, `meta-roi-framework`, `meta-revenue-planning`, `meta-utm-tracking`, `meta-dashboard-design`, `meta-testing-framework`, and related skills create an optimisation and accountability loop.

The repository’s implied reporting cycle is:

1. Define metrics that match business goals.
2. Build tracking, naming, and attribution discipline.
3. Report performance in an insight-led way.
4. Link platform outputs to inquiries, revenue logic, and investment cases.
5. Use testing and optimisation frameworks to improve results over time.

## Retention And Growth

Retention is explicitly present. `playbook-client-retainer-management`, `playbook-agency-operations`, quarterly review decks, annual review decks, and ROI/business-case skills collectively support:

- Scope control.
- Renewal conversations.
- Monthly and quarterly review rituals.
- Expansion into additional services.
- More disciplined client communications.

This means the system does not end at deliverable creation; it includes the mechanics of sustaining client relationships profitably.

## AI Layer

The AI layer is unusually mature for a marketing repository. It is not limited to prompt templates. It includes readiness diagnostics, use-case mapping, vendor evaluation, workflow automation, brand voice training, humanisation, knowledge-base design, chatbot planning, and AI ethics/IP policy.

That gives the system three advantages:

- Productivity support.
- Better consistency across content and strategy outputs.
- Governance against low-quality or unsafe AI use.

## What A Real Consultant Could Do With It

A solo consultant or small agency could use this repository to run most of the knowledge and document side of the business:

- Position the firm.
- Prospect and pitch.
- Onboard clients consistently.
- Produce strategy and campaign documents.
- Manage community, content, reporting, and review cycles.
- Build renewal and growth conversations around measurable performance.

The practical constraint is that the repository intentionally excludes some production and execution layers, especially design, video, web implementation, and ad-ops management. So it is best understood as a high-quality consultancy operating system for strategy, planning, operational guidance, and reporting, rather than a fully self-contained service delivery factory.
