## 1. The Three-Brain Model

The audience processes your pitch through three brain layers in sequence:

| Brain Layer | Function | What It Needs From You |
|---|---|---|
| Crocodile brain (oldest) | Survival, threat detection — first filter | Simple, clear, non-threatening, novel |
| Midbrain | Social meaning, status, context | Status and social relevance |
| Neocortex (newest) | Logic, problem-solving, detail | Data, analysis, reason |

**The fundamental problem:** You compose your pitch from your neocortex. The audience receives it in their crocodile brain. Up to 90% of your message is discarded before reaching the neocortex.

**Crocodile brain filtering rules:**
1. If it is not dangerous, ignore it
2. If it is not new and exciting, ignore it
3. If it is new, summarise it fast and forget the details
4. Only escalate to the neocortex if genuinely unexpected

**Implications for every slide:** Satisfy the croc brain first — simple, high-contrast, novel. Logic follows only after the croc brain has passed the message up.

## 2. The STRONG Method

Klaff's six-step pitch sequence:

| Step | What It Means | How to Apply |
|---|---|---|
| **S** — Setting the Frame | Establish context and authority before speaking | Open as a peer, not a supplicant — do not thank them for their time |
| **T** — Telling the Story | Brief, vivid narrative introducing the idea with novelty | Early slides establish the client's situation with energy |
| **R** — Revealing the Intrigue | Short provocative information that arouses curiosity | The "why now" or opportunity cost moment — novel, specific, unexpected |
| **O** — Offering the Prize | You are the desirable resource — create desire, do not chase | The concept or approach slides — present with confidence, not apology |
| **N** — Nailing the Hookpoint | Audience transitions from interested to involved — they ask questions | Investment and scope slides — they start calculating value |
| **G** — Getting the Decision | Close with a clear binary decision point | Next steps slide — a specific, dated ask |

## 3. Frame Control

A frame is the context and authority you bring into a room. When two frames meet, one wins.

**Four frames you will face:**

| Frame Type | How It Appears | Your Counter |
|---|---|---|
| **Power frame** | Arrogance, dismissiveness — they act as if they are doing you a favour | Power-busting: minor, playful non-compliance. Do not defer. |
| **Time frame** | "I only have 10 minutes" | Be the one who ends the meeting, not them |
| **Analyst frame** | "Show me only the numbers" | Inject an intrigue ping — an incomplete story that demands full attention |
| **Prize frame** | You hold this; they want access | Maintain throughout: confident, not needy |

**Key rule:** If you have to justify your authority, you have already lost the frame collision.

---

## 4. Prize Framing Rules

- Behave as the resource the client is trying to access — not the vendor trying to close
- Do not chase — chasing signals low status and low value
- Maximum two options — the crocodile brain cannot process complex multi-option decisions
- Scarcity is only credible when demonstrated, not stated
- Never be needy — neediness triggers rejection before logic can engage

---

## 5. Status Principles

- Arrive as a peer or superior — never as a supplicant
- Do not open by thanking them for their time
- Find something in their environment to comment on or mildly improve — signals your frame is stronger
- Strong frames never need justification
