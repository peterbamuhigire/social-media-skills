## 1. The Presenter as Mentor

The audience is the hero. The presenter is the mentor — like Yoda, not Luke Skywalker. The presenter gives the hero a gift, a tool, or a path forward.

Filter every slide through: "What does this audience need from me?" — not "What do I want to tell them?"

**Implication for pitch decks:** The client's name, goals, and language should appear far more often than the agency's name and credentials.

## 2. The Big Idea

Every presentation must be built around a single Big Idea — a complete sentence with:
- A **point of view** (a stance, not a topic label)
- **What is at stake** for the audience if they do not act

| Weak (label) | Strong (Big Idea with stance) |
|---|---|
| "Social media strategy" | "Your competitors are building customer relationships on social media while your brand goes unnoticed — every month you wait costs you market share" |
| "Platform performance" | "Three platforms generate 80% of all enquiries — we are only active on one of them" |
| "Investment" | "This engagement costs less per month than one lost customer" |

**Slide title discipline:** Every slide title must be a complete assertion, never a label. Audiences scan titles like news headlines — make each one carry the argument.

## 3. The Sparkline

The structural backbone of the most persuasive presentations. Oscillates between two states:

```
Opening:  WHAT IS (current reality — audience nods in recognition)
          ↓ gap created ↓
          WHAT COULD BE (the better future — audience leans forward)

Middle:   Toggle: WHAT IS (tension) / WHAT COULD BE (release)
          Repeat multiple times, building the case with each cycle

Close:    WHAT COULD BE (vivid, specific picture of the new bliss)
          + Call to Action (the bridge to get there)
```

**The gap between What Is and What Could Be creates productive tension.** Do not resolve it until the call to action.

**Application in deck-initial-pitch:**
- Slide 3 (What We Heard) = What Is — mirrors back the client's current reality
- Slide 4 (The Gap) = What Is vs. What Could Be contrast — makes the gap visceral
- Slide 5 (Opportunity Cost) = What Is without action — stakes of staying still
- Slide 6 (Recommended Approach) = What Could Be direction — first full picture of the better future
- Slides 9–14 (Scope, Timeline, Investment) = The route from What Is to What Could Be
- Slide 14 (Next Steps) = Call to Action — the decision that resolves the tension

## 4. The S.T.A.R. Moment

Every great presentation needs one climactic, memorable moment that drives the Big Idea home. Plant it at the highest-energy slide — typically Slide 5 (Opportunity Cost) or at the close.

Four types:
- **S — Shocking statistics:** Amplify, do not gloss over. Display the number large. Let it sit in silence for three seconds.
- **T — Transformative visuals:** An emotionally potent image that makes the abstract concrete
- **A — Acting it out:** A prop, live demonstration, physical metaphor, or unexpected visual analogy
- **R — Relatable anecdote:** A personal or client story with vulnerability and a clear lesson

**One S.T.A.R. moment per deck** — attempting multiple dilutes the impact.

## 5. Call to Action Taxonomy

Be explicit about which action you need from this specific audience:

| Type | Who They Are | What You Ask Them to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Doers** | Decision-makers with direct authority | Sign the agreement, approve the scope, schedule the kickoff |
| **Suppliers** | Those who control resources | Approve the budget, release the brand assets, provide access |
| **Influencers** | Those who advocate internally | Champion the proposal to the board, brief other stakeholders |
| **Innovators** | Those who shape the brief | Feedback on the approach, revisions to scope |

Identify before presenting which type is in the room — and tailor the close to that specific action.

## 6. The Glance Test

Every slide must be comprehensible to a new viewer in three seconds.

If it fails:
- Too much text → reduce to one assertion + three bullets maximum
- Ambiguous visual → replace with a single, clear image or chart
- No clear headline → rewrite the title as a complete assertion

**The slide's job is to anchor the message. The presenter's job is to explain it.** Slides that explain themselves are doing the presenter's job — and doing it poorly.

## 7. Two Strategic Questions (Hatton)

Before preparing any presentation, answer these two questions in one sentence each:

1. **Why should they say yes?** — This defines your content strategy: the relevant benefits and competitive differentiation to lead with
2. **Why will they remember this meeting?** — This defines your delivery strategy: the one thing that will make this presentation distinctive

When both questions are answered, you have a strategy. When neither is answered, you have a slide deck.
