## 1. The Three-Brain Model

The audience processes your pitch through three brain layers in sequence:

| Brain Layer | Function | What It Needs From You |
|---|---|---|
| Crocodile brain (oldest) | Survival, threat detection — first filter | Simple, clear, non-threatening, novel |
| Midbrain | Social meaning, status, context | Status and social relevance |
| Neocortex (newest) | Logic, problem-solving, detail | Data, analysis, reason |

**The fundamental problem:** You compose your pitch from your neocortex. The audience receives it in their crocodile brain. Up to 90% of your message is discarded before reaching the neocortex.

**Crocodile brain filtering rules:**
1. If it is not dangerous, ignore it
2. If it is not new and exciting, ignore it
3. If it is new, summarise it fast and forget the details
4. Only escalate to the neocortex if genuinely unexpected

**Implications for every slide:** Satisfy the croc brain first — simple, high-contrast, novel. Logic follows only after the croc brain has passed the message up.

## 2. The STRONG Method

Klaff's six-step pitch sequence — maps directly onto the deck-initial-pitch slide structure:

| Step | What It Means | Slides |
|---|---|---|
| **S** — Setting the Frame | Establish context and authority before speaking — own the room | Slides 1–2 |
| **T** — Telling the Story | Brief, vivid narrative to introduce the client's situation with novelty | Slides 3–4 |
| **R** — Revealing the Intrigue | Short provocative information that arouses curiosity and keeps attention | Slide 5 (opportunity cost) |
| **O** — Offering the Prize | You are the desirable resource — create desire, do not chase | Slides 6–8 |
| **N** — Nailing the Hookpoint | The audience transitions from interested to involved — they ask questions | Slides 9–11 |
| **G** — Getting the Decision | Close with a clear binary decision point | Slides 14–15 |

## 3. Frame Control

A frame is the context and authority you bring into a room. When two frames meet, one wins and one is absorbed. You cannot share frames.

**Four frames you will face:**

| Frame Type | How It Appears | Your Counter |
|---|---|---|
| **Power frame** | Arrogance, dismissiveness, entitlement — they act as if they are doing you a favour | Power-busting frame: introduce a minor, playful act of non-compliance. Do not defer. |
| **Time frame** | "I only have 10 minutes" — establishes time as their resource, not yours | Time-constraining counter: be the one who ends the meeting, not them. "Let me give you the overview in 5 minutes — then we can decide if it makes sense to go deeper." |
| **Analyst frame** | "Show me only the numbers" — attempts to reduce you to a data presenter | Intrigue frame: inject a puzzle or incomplete story that demands their full attention. |
| **Prize frame** | You hold this; you are the scarce, desirable resource | Maintain it throughout: confident, not needy, not chasing |

**Key rule:** If you have to justify your authority, status, or relevance, you have already lost the frame collision. Strong frames never need justification.

## 4. Prize Framing Rules

- Behave as if you are the resource the client is trying to access — not the salesperson trying to close
- Do not chase: each time you chase, you signal low status and low value
- Maximum two options: the crocodile brain cannot process complex multi-option decisions
- Scarcity is only credible when demonstrated, not stated — reference real capacity constraints
- Never be needy: neediness triggers rejection at the croc-brain level before logic can engage
- One effective prize frame technique: "We typically take on one new client per quarter in each sector — we are currently evaluating two other businesses in your category"

---

## 5. Status Principles

- Arrive as a peer or superior — never as a supplicant
- Do not open by thanking them for their time — this signals you need them more than they need you
- Find something in their environment or sector to comment on or mildly improve — this signals your frame is stronger
- Strong frames never need justification
- The most powerful status signal: you are so confident in the outcome that you are willing to ask hard questions and challenge assumptions before the engagement begins
