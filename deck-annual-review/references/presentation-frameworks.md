## 1. The Big Idea Per Slide

Every slide title is a complete, active assertion — not a topic label. Audiences scan titles like news headlines — each title must carry the argument.

| Weak (label) | Strong (assertion) |
|---|---|
| "Platform performance" | "Our three platforms generate 80% of all enquiries" |
| "Engagement rate" | "Engagement rate is 2× the industry benchmark this quarter" |
| "Content strategy" | "Consistent content builds audiences — here is the 90-day schedule" |
| "Results" | "Month 3 exceeded every KPI we set at the strategy presentation" |
| "Next steps" | "If you approve today, Month 2 content goes live on [date]" |

Apply this discipline to every slide in every deck — a deck of complete assertions tells a story; a deck of labels is an index.

---

## 2. The Glance Test

Every slide must be comprehensible to a new viewer in three seconds. If it fails:
- Too much text → reduce to one assertion + three bullets maximum
- Ambiguous visual → replace with a single, clear image or chart
- No clear message → rewrite the title as a complete assertion

The slide's job is to anchor the message. The presenter's job is to explain it.

## 3. The Executive 10% Rule

When presenting to senior decision-makers or business owners:
- Lead with conclusions, not evidence — bottom line first
- Executive summary slides ≈ 10% of the total deck (e.g. 1–2 summary slides for a 12-slide deck)
- State upfront: "I will take three minutes on the summary, then open for questions"
- Let the audience drive into detail — do not front-load analysis
- The most important insight from any reporting deck must appear in the first two slides

## 4. Audience Segmentation (Duarte's Five Lenses)

Before writing slides, profile the primary decision-maker in the room:
1. **Politics** — who has veto power? who influences the decision?
2. **Demographics** — age, education, familiarity with social media metrics
3. **Psychographics** — what do they value? what concerns them most?
4. **Firmographics** — business size, revenue, growth stage, sector
5. **Ethnographics** — cultural context and communication norms (critical in Uganda/East Africa)

Practical rule: identify the one most influential person in the room and write the deck primarily for them.

## 5. The Sparkline (Adapted for Strategy and Reporting Decks)

The Sparkline structure — oscillating between What Is and What Could Be — applies to strategy and reporting contexts:

**Strategy decks:**
- What Is = the audit findings and current baseline
- What Could Be = the strategy outcomes and 90-day targets
- The strategy statement slide is the first full expression of What Could Be

**Reporting decks:**
- What Is = the results this period
- What Could Be = the optimised trajectory if recommendations are implemented
- End on a forward-looking slide that reconnects to What Could Be, not a backward-looking data summary

Never end a reporting deck on historical data. Close with the next action.

## 6. The Empathy Model (Hatton)

The client is the hero. Demonstrate genuine understanding by:
- Opening with what matters most to the client, not what took the most effort to produce
- Using their language and terminology — not agency jargon or platform-specific acronyms they do not use
- Framing every data point in terms of what it means for their business: "This means you received 40 more enquiries than last month" not "Reach increased 18%"
- Referencing specific pressures in their sector and market — a Kampala restaurant has different concerns from a Nairobi tech firm
- Addressing the decision-maker's individual agenda, not just the company's goals

## 7. The Persuasion Sandwich (Hatton)

For strategy and reporting decks, structure the overall deck as:

| Section | Purpose | Example slides |
|---|---|---|
| **Top slice** | Why this matters — frame the session around the client's goals | Agenda, audit findings, strategy statement |
| **Filling** | The content — data, findings, recommendations, plans — benefits-led | Platform selection, content pillars, KPIs, roadmap |
| **Bottom slice** | Why act now — connect findings to decisions; close with next steps | Investment, next steps, Q&A |

**Critical rule:** Never end on a data slide. The final impression is as important as the first. Close with a decision-prompt or forward-looking slide.

---

## 8. Two Strategic Questions (Hatton)

Before preparing any presentation, answer these two questions in one sentence each:
1. **Why should they say yes?** — defines content strategy (which benefits and recommendations to lead with)
2. **Why will they remember this presentation?** — defines delivery strategy (the one distinctive moment or insight)

When both questions are answered, you have a strategy. A deck without answers to both questions is a slide collection.
